The Urgent Case for Essays: Reclaiming Your Mind in the Age of Fake Thinking

We are living through an unprecedented era of information overload, where the very fabric of our ability to think is being eroded. The constant barrage of short-form content, algorithmically driven engagement, and AI-generated text is creating a "fake thinking epidemic" with profound consequences, not just for individuals, but for civilization itself. In this environment, the humble essay emerges not as a relic of the past, but as a vital tool for learning faster, thinking deeper, and preserving our cognitive capacity.

The Internet Isn't Dead, But It's Killing Us

The modern information landscape, dominated by social media and AI, poses a direct threat to our collective ability to make sense of reality. As Daniel Schmachtenberger observes, "The written word as the primary type of media was probably required for democracy to work because it required the capacity to pay attention to an idea for long enough to understand it." Today, the constant stream of bite-sized, often polarizing content on platforms like Instagram, TikTok, and X (formerly Twitter) conditions us to skim, react, and avoid deep engagement.

This isn't just about individual distraction; it's about the poisoning of the "epistemic commons" – the shared reservoir of information we rely on. When the content we consume and create prioritizes engagement over truth or transformation, our intellectual water supply becomes contaminated. This contamination directly influences our identity, behavior, and ultimately, the trajectory of our lives. Furthermore, the form of information we consume trains our attention spans, our tolerance for complexity, and our capacity for nuance. Consuming short-form, low-effort content atrophies these crucial cognitive muscles, leaving us ill-equipped to tackle complex civilizational challenges like climate change, AI alignment, or public health.

The Three Forces Breaking Civilization's Ability to Think

Daniel Schmachtenberger identifies three converging forces that threaten to lead civilization towards catastrophic outcomes:

  1. Rivalrous Dynamics: These are win-lose games where one party's gain necessitates another's loss. Examples include arms races, cutthroat corporate competition, and the relentless pursuit of engagement in the content creation space, where creators often abandon truth for virality.
  2. Substrate Consumption: This occurs when systems consume their foundational elements faster than they can regenerate. The attention economy, for instance, consumes human cognitive capacity at an alarming rate, depleting our ability to focus and think deeply.
  3. Exponential Technology: Tools and systems that improve at accelerating rates, outpacing human wisdom. AI's rapid advancement, automated weapons, and ever-evolving social media algorithms fall into this category.

When these forces converge, they can lead to civilizational collapse (through nuclear war, unaligned AI, ecological destruction, or engineered pandemics) or dystopian control (through total surveillance and digital authoritarianism). The alternative, a positive outcome, requires the existence of robust sensemaking, shared understanding, and aligned incentives.

The current internet ecosystem exacerbates these threats. Content creators optimize for engagement, leading to the proliferation of shallow, unthinking content. AI, while powerful, accelerates imitation, and when the imitable content is destructive, the epistemic commons becomes poisoned. The issue isn't AI itself, but its ability to mimic thought without requiring cognitive effort from either creator or consumer.

Why Essays May Be the Last Bastion of Real Thinking

In contrast to the "fast food for the mind" offered by social media, essays represent "slow content" – information that requires and fosters deep thinking. While Buzzfeed listicles, rage tweets, and AI-generated summaries offer immediate gratification, essays demand engagement, reflection, and potentially, a shift in perspective.

An essay is not merely an article presenting existing knowledge; it is an argument, an act of thinking, and a discovery process. It's where the author refines their beliefs, grapples with complexity, and arrives at a conclusion through a process of genuine inquiry. This is a uniquely human endeavor. AI, lacking situated experience, beliefs, biases, and emotions, cannot replicate the authentic point of view that forms the core of a meaningful essay. Your unique experiences, memories, and unconscious drivers are the raw material that AI cannot access.

The act of writing an essay forces you to confront your own ideas, to articulate them clearly, and to subject them to potential criticism. This process develops your thinking capacity and can influence thousands of people across generations. Many of history's most influential thinkers, from Isaac Newton to Emerson, honed their ideas through essay writing.

The Meaning Economy and How to Thrive In It

We are entering a "meaning economy," where meaning itself is the scarcest and most valuable commodity. In an age of abundance in material goods and information, many people feel empty, purposeless, and disconnected. They crave meaning, which is not found but created through the ordering of consciousness.

Psychic entropy, characterized by anxiety and restlessness, arises from fragmented attention. Psychic negropy, the experience of flow, purpose, and meaning, emerges when attention is invested in complex, challenging activities with clear feedback. Meaning is the experience of ordered consciousness, and it is created through the process of wrestling with complexity until it coheres.

Fast content, by delivering pre-packaged conclusions, skips this ordering process, leaving the consumer informed but empty. Slow content, like essays, requires both the writer and the reader to engage in this ordering process. The writer orders their own consciousness through writing, and the reader reorders their thoughts by digesting the content.

The opportunity lies in becoming a "value creator" – someone who makes sense of their own minds and documents that journey publicly. These individuals don't chase fleeting trends or build massive tech companies; they provide the foundational information that shapes identity and behavior, ultimately influencing the flourishing or destruction of civilization. This is a deeply meaningful and future-proof way to live.

How to Think in Public (and Why It's the Most Valuable Skill You Can Develop)

The act of thinking in public, particularly through essay writing, is arguably the most valuable skill one can develop today. It's a challenging process, but one that leads to personal growth and the creation of meaningful work.

To begin writing your first essay:

  1. Write to Discover, Not to Perform: Start with a concept, question, or experience that genuinely interests you. Embrace uncertainty and an open mind.
  2. Focus on Genuine Interest: Dive deep into a single main idea. Research, challenge assumptions, and don't accept any single source as absolute truth.
  3. Resist the Template: Allow your writing structure to evolve naturally. Engage in a debate with yourself, asking questions to drive the writing forward. Worry about formal structure later.
  4. Ask, "Do I Actually Believe This?": The most difficult but crucial step is to challenge your own beliefs and resist the urge to present yourself as infallible.
  5. Consume Centropic Content: Actively seek out and curate content that fosters deep thinking, as the "for you page" is unlikely to provide it.
  6. Build a Body of Work: Focus on creating a coherent collection of essays that build upon each other, rather than adhering to a rigid content calendar. AI cannot replicate a genuine philosophy built through years of lived experience and thought.

Platforms like X (formerly Twitter) and Substack are currently seeing a revival of long-form content and are excellent places to start publishing your work. Substack, in particular, offers ownership of your audience, an email-first approach, and a notes feed that values deep thinking.

By embracing the practice of writing essays, you can reclaim your cognitive capacity, contribute meaningfully to the public discourse, and thrive in the emerging meaning economy.
