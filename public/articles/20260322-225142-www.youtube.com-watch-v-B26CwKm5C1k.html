# Engineering in the AI Era: A Conversation with OpenAI's Sherwin Wu

Sherwin Wu, the head of engineering for OpenAI's API and developer platform, has a unique vantage point into the rapidly evolving world of AI. He oversees the platform that powers countless AI startups and has a front-row seat to how AI is transforming the landscape of software development and business itself. In this conversation, he shares insights on AI's role in coding, the future of engineering, and the second-order effects of a world powered by intelligent agents.

## AI’s Role in Coding at OpenAI

The adoption of AI tools within OpenAI has been transformative. According to Wu, approximately 95% of engineers use **Codex**, their AI-powered coding assistant, on a daily basis. The impact is significant, with 100% of pull requests (PRs) being reviewed by Codex before they are merged. This integration has not only changed workflows but also boosted productivity.

Engineers who actively use Codex are opening 70% more PRs than those who don't, and this gap is widening. The initial generated code is almost entirely from AI, which engineers then review and refine. This shift has created a tangible energy within the company as developers continually discover new capabilities and their trust in the models grows.

Wu references a quote from Kevin Whale, OpenAI's VP of Science: "This is the worst the models will ever be." This sentiment captures the rapid pace of improvement, suggesting that the capabilities and reliability of AI tools for software engineering will only increase, leading to even deeper integration and trust over time.

## The Future of Software Engineering with AI

The job of a software engineer is undergoing one of the most significant transformations in its history. Engineers are evolving from writing every line of code to becoming more like tech leads or managers of "fleets of agents." They steer numerous parallel threads, providing feedback and direction to AI assistants like Codex.

Wu draws a powerful metaphor from the classic programming textbook, *Structure and Interpretation of Computer Programs* (SICP), which describes programmers as wizards casting spells. He notes that with modern AI, this analogy has become literal. Engineers now use natural language as incantations to command the computer, making the process feel like magic.

This new paradigm is reminiscent of the **Sorcerer's Apprentice**, where immense power is available but requires skill and oversight to manage. A senior engineer proficient with these tools can achieve incredible leverage, but they must remain vigilant to ensure the AI agents don't go "off the rails." The result is a more dynamic and, for many, a more enjoyable role focused on higher-level strategy and problem-solving.

### The Stress of Managing Agents

This new way of working isn't without its challenges. A new form of stress has emerged among developers when their AI agents fail to perform as expected. When an agent can't accomplish a task, the engineer must figure out how to guide it correctly.

To understand this better, an internal team at OpenAI is experimenting with maintaining a 100% Codex-written codebase, removing the "escape hatch" of manual coding. They've found that when an agent struggles, the root cause is often a lack of **context**. The solution is not to take over, but to provide the agent with better information, whether through documentation, code comments, or other resources that encode the necessary tribal knowledge.

### Code Review and Automation

With AI accelerating code production, code review could easily become a bottleneck. However, Wu explains that Codex also automates this part of the process. By reviewing 100% of PRs, Codex turns what used to be a 15-minute task into a two or three-minute one. For smaller changes, developers often trust Codex's review entirely, freeing up human engineers to focus elsewhere.

This automation extends beyond code review. The entire CI/CD process, including fixing linting errors and managing deployments, is being streamlined with AI, allowing developers to merge and ship code much faster.

## The Changing Role of Engineering Managers

While the role of an IC engineer has changed dramatically, the manager's role is also evolving. Wu notes that AI tools like Codex empower top performers to become exceptionally productive. His management philosophy has always been to spend the majority of his time—more than 50%—with his top performers to ensure they are unblocked and happy. In the AI era, he believes this is more important than ever.

Wu also foresees that AI will enable managers to lead much larger teams effectively. By using AI to synthesize organizational knowledge, track progress, and even assist with performance reviews, a manager's leverage increases significantly. This could shift the conventional wisdom of a manager having six to eight direct reports.

## The One-Person Billion-Dollar Startup

The idea of a **one-person billion-dollar startup** has captured the imagination of the tech world. While Wu finds the concept fascinating, he believes people aren't fully pricing in the second and third-order effects. If it's possible for one person to build such a company, it also means it's vastly easier for anyone to create a startup.

This could lead to a golden age of **B2B SaaS**, with a proliferation of small, highly specialized startups building bespoke software for niche markets. To enable one billion-dollar company, there might be a hundred smaller startups providing the specific tools it needs. This explosion of smaller, successful businesses could fundamentally change the startup and VC ecosystems, prioritizing individual success over venture-scale returns.

## Navigating AI Deployments and Customer Feedback

Many companies struggle to achieve a positive ROI on their AI deployments. Wu observes that this often happens when there's a top-down mandate to "use AI" without genuine bottoms-up adoption. The employees who are meant to use the tools don't understand the technology and have no one to learn from.

His advice is to create a **"tiger team"** of enthusiastic internal evangelists. This cross-functional group can explore the technology's capabilities, apply it to specific workflows, and share knowledge to generate excitement and practical best practices throughout the organization.

In the fast-moving field of AI, Wu also cautions against blindly listening to customers. "The models will eat your scaffolding for breakfast," he says, meaning that the foundational tools and frameworks built around today's models may quickly become obsolete as the models themselves improve. For example, the focus on vector stores in 2023 has already shifted as models become better at using simpler search tools and context files.

## Building for the Future of AI

The key takeaway for builders in the AI space is to **build for where the models are going, not where they are today**. Startups that anticipate the next wave of capabilities and design their products accordingly are the ones most likely to succeed. When the models catch up, their product "clicks" and becomes incredibly powerful.

So, where are the models headed in the next 12 to 18 months?

*   **Longer, Coherent Tasks:** Models will become capable of performing multi-hour tasks coherently, expanding the universe of what can be automated.
*   **Improved Multimodality:** Audio, in particular, will see significant advancements. Given how much business is conducted through speech, this is a hugely underrated domain with massive potential.

Wu is also bullish on **Business Process Automation (BPA)**. Outside the tech bubble, much of the world's work consists of repeatable, operational tasks. He believes there's a massive, underrated opportunity to apply AI to automate these processes, which could have a profound impact on the broader economy.

## OpenAI’s Platform Strategy and Mission

For startups worried about being squashed by OpenAI, Wu emphasizes that OpenAI fundamentally views itself as an **ecosystem platform company**. Its mission is not just to build AGI but to spread its benefits to all of humanity, which requires a thriving ecosystem of third-party developers.

The market is vast, and the opportunity is unprecedented. Wu's advice to startups is simple: "Don't overly stress about this. Just build something that people like, and you will have a space." He points out that startups are more likely to fail because of a lack of product-market fit than because of competition from a major lab. A rising tide lifts all boats, and OpenAI is committed to raising the tide for everyone.
