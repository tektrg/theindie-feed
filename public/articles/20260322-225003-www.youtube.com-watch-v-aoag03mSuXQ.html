In 2021, a sophisticated hacker discovered a critical weakness in the world's most dominant operating system. This vulnerability was weeks away from granting attackers a master key to millions of internet servers, creating a potential for espionage, ransomware, or even crippling entire nations. This is the story of how a jammed printer led to a global software movement, and how that movement's greatest strength nearly became its downfall.

The Jammed Printer and the Birth of Free Software

In the early days of the MIT AI lab, a new Xerox 9700 laser printer was a big deal, but it had a persistent problem: it kept jamming. For researcher Richard Stallman, this was a frustrating waste of time. Years earlier, he had solved a similar issue by programming an alert system that notified users of a jam. He intended to do the same now, but there was a problem.

Xerox had not provided the source code for the printer. Without it, Stallman couldn't modify the software. When he contacted the developer who wrote the code, his request for a copy was denied.

"He said, 'No, I promised not to give you a copy.' I was stunned. I was angry," Stallman recalled. "I realized that I was seeing not just an isolated jerk but a social phenomenon that was important and affected a lot of people."

This phenomenon was the shift towards proprietary software. In the late 1960s, AT&T's Bell Labs had invented the Unix operating system and shared it widely. But by the 80s, the landscape had changed. Companies began enforcing copyrights and requiring programmers to sign non-disclosure agreements (NDAs), preventing them from sharing code.

Stallman was faced with a choice: adapt to this new, closed-off world or forge a different path. He chose the latter.

"I have spent my life building walls to divide people," he imagined saying at the end of a career in proprietary software. "And I would've been ashamed of my life."

In 1985, Stallman quit his job at MIT and established the Free Software Foundation. Its mission was to promote four basic freedoms for software users:

  • The freedom to run the software for any purpose.
  • The freedom to study how it works and change it.
  • The freedom to share copies.
  • The freedom to distribute your modified versions to others.

To legally protect these freedoms, he created the General Public License (GPL). He also began the GNU project—a recursive acronym for "GNU's Not Unix"—to build an entire operating system from the ground up that would be completely free.

The GNU project successfully recreated the essential tools and the command-line shell, but after seven years, it was still missing one crucial component: the kernel, the core of the operating system that communicates directly with the hardware.

The Missing Piece: Linux is Born

In 1991, Stallman gave a talk at the University of Helsinki. In the audience was a computer science student named Linus Torvalds, who happened to be building his own kernel. Inspired by Stallman's vision, Torvalds decided to release his kernel under the General Public License. He named it Linux.

The combination of the Linux kernel with the tools from the GNU project created a complete, free operating system. While "Linux" technically refers only to the kernel, the term is now commonly used to describe the entire system. This new model of open-source development, where anyone could inspect the code, improve it, and fix flaws, was revolutionary.

The Rise of an Open-Source Behemoth

Software development split into two competing ideologies: proprietary, closed-source systems controlled by companies, and free, open-source projects. The freedom to view and modify the code proved to be a significant advantage for Linux.

"Engineers are just itching to just fix it themselves," one developer noted. "They didn't have to reinvent the wheel every time."

This adaptability allowed Linux to spread everywhere. Today, it is the most popular operating system in the world, dwarfing Windows and macOS.

  • Every one of the world's top 500 supercomputers runs Linux.
  • It is used in the Pentagon, on US nuclear submarines, and by banks, hospitals, and governments.
  • Android, which runs on over 3 billion devices, is built on the Linux kernel.
  • The majority of the world's internet servers are powered by Linux.

The entire system, however, relies on one key assumption: that the code is secure. This belief is codified in Linus's Law: "Given enough eyeballs, all bugs are shallow." The idea is that with so many people looking at the code, any flaw or backdoor will be caught quickly.

A Crack in the Foundation

The problem with Linus's Law is that the open-source movement isn't one monolithic project. It's an ecosystem of thousands of smaller tools and libraries, each maintained by different people. Many of these critical components are passion projects, built and maintained by unpaid volunteers in their spare time. As a popular XKCD comic illustrates, the entire modern digital infrastructure can rest on a single, thanklessly maintained project run by one person.

This is where our story's vulnerability begins. Lasse Collin, a developer from Finland, had been maintaining a small but highly effective data compression tool called XZ since 2005. XZ was so good that it was eventually adopted by nearly every major Linux distribution. For almost two decades, the work of maintaining the tool fell almost entirely on Collin, who was never paid for his efforts.

By the 2020s, he was facing immense pressure and burnout. Users on mailing lists complained about the slow pace of updates, unaware of his personal struggles.

"I haven't lost interest, but my ability to care has been fairly limited, mostly due to long-term mental health issues," Lasse responded. "It's also good to keep in mind that this is an unpaid hobby project."

Just as he was reaching his breaking point, a new contributor appeared: Jia Tan. He was incredibly helpful, taking on tasks and submitting patches. Eventually, he offered to take over as maintainer of the XZ project. For Collin, it seemed too good to be true. He handed over control, finally able to step back after 20 years.

But Jia Tan was not who he appeared to be. He had identified Lasse Collin's XZ project as a weak link—a dependency that could give him a backdoor into almost every computer on the internet.

The Master Key to the Internet: SSH

To understand the hacker's goal, one must first understand Secure Shell (SSH). Born from a 1995 password-sniffing attack at a university, SSH was created to solve two fundamental problems of remote login:

  1. Securing the channel: Computers must agree on a secret code to scramble their data. They do this over an insecure network using a method similar to mixing paints. Both sides agree on a public "color" (a large number), mix in their own private "color," and exchange the results. By combining the received mixture with their own private color again, both arrive at the same final secret "color" without ever having shared their private ingredients. This process, known as the Diffie-Hellman key exchange, creates an encrypted channel.

  2. Authenticating the user: To prevent a "man-in-the-middle" attack, where a hacker sits between two users and relays messages, the server must prove its identity. This is done using RSA encryption. The server has a secret private key and a public key. A client uses the public key to scramble a message that only the server's private key can unscramble. This authenticates the server and foils any impostor.

Today, OpenSSH is the most widely used SSH implementation and the maintenance backbone of the entire internet. It is one of the most heavily scrutinized projects in the world. Gaining a backdoor into OpenSSH would be like having a master key to every hotel room.

Jia Tan knew hacking OpenSSH directly was impossible. Instead, he targeted one of its many dependencies: Lasse Collin's compression library, XZ.

The Anatomy of a Sophisticated Hack

Over two and a half years, Jia Tan executed a meticulous, multi-stage plan to insert a backdoor.

Step 1: The Trojan Horse

Jia couldn't add the malicious code directly to the source, as it would be too obvious. Instead, he hid his payload inside binary test files. These files are used to test compression algorithms and are seen as blobs of meaningless data, not human-readable code. He then made a small, easy-to-miss change to the project's build script, which quietly unpacked the payload from the test files and inserted it into the final XZ library.

Step 2: Goldilocks

The hacker's goal was to hijack the RSA authentication step within SSH. To do this, he needed to modify the Global Offset Table (GOT), a table that tells a program where to find functions in shared libraries. He wanted to replace the address of the real RSA decryption function with the address of his own malicious code.

However, there was only a tiny window to do this. He had to act after the system legitimately wrote the real address, but before the system locked the table as "Read-Only." To hit this "Goldilocks zone," he used two obscure tools:

  • An IFUNC resolver, a feature for optimizing code for different hardware, allowed him to run his own code very early in the program's execution.
  • A dynamic audit hook, a debugging feature, allowed him to trigger his code at the exact moment the linker wrote the RSA function's address into the table.

The IFUNC set the hook, and when the real RSA function was loaded, the hook fired, swapping in the address of Jia's payload right in the middle of the Goldilocks zone.

Step 3: The Cat Burglar

With the exploit in place, SSH wasn't just checking for a legitimate login anymore; it was also listening for a hidden master key. This master key was itself protected by a mini cryptographic exchange, ensuring only the attacker could use the backdoor. To cover his tracks, the payload was designed to wipe any evidence from SSH logs and included numerous safety checks to prevent crashes that might draw attention.

The trap was set. Cautious, meticulous, and designed to run invisibly.

Discovery by Chance

Just as Jia's compromised code was being added to pre-release versions of major Linux distributions like Fedora and Debian, the entire plot was unraveled by a single person's curiosity.

Andres Freund, a Microsoft programmer working on an unrelated database project called Postgres, was running performance tests. He noticed a tiny, consistent slowdown—about half a second—when connecting to a server over SSH. It wasn't much, but it was enough to make him suspicious.

He dug deeper, tracing the delay back to the recent update in the XZ library. He saw the strange binary test files and the convoluted build scripts. He couldn't shake the feeling that something was wrong.

"I remember sitting in a bunch of meetings and not really being able to concentrate because it feels like, I need to continue looking into this," Freund said.

Eventually, he saw it. This wasn't a bug; it was a backdoor. Ironically, the attacker's extreme caution—the layers of obfuscation, custom decoders, and safety checks—was what created the very performance lag that caught Freund's attention. Had the attack been less complex, it likely would have gone unnoticed.

On March 29, 2024, Freund posted his findings to a public security mailing list. All hell broke loose. Red Hat, Debian, and other distributors scrambled to roll back the malicious code and alert users. The open-source community began a deep dive to understand what had happened. Andres Freund was hailed as a hero who single-handedly saved the internet from a potential catastrophe.

Who is Jia Tan?

The identity of Jia Tan and the group behind the attack remains a mystery. The pressure campaign on Lasse Collin involved multiple "sock puppet" accounts with no history outside the XZ mailing lists. While timestamps for code commits often aligned with Beijing's time zone (UTC+8), the attackers also worked on Chinese New Year and sometimes on a schedule that aligned with time zones in Western Russia or Israel.

The level of patience, sophistication, and resources—a campaign running for over two and a half years—strongly suggests a nation-state actor rather than a criminal organization.

"Who spends a million dollars and takes two and a half years to attempt to break into every hotel room on the internet with a master key?" one analyst wondered. "I think it has to be a nation-state actor."

Speculation has pointed to groups like China's intelligence services or Russia's APT29, also known as Cozy Bear. However, the clues could easily be intentional misdirection. Jia Tan's accounts vanished the moment the exploit became public, and he has not been heard from since.

A Victory for Open Source?

The XZ backdoor incident has sparked a debate about the security of the open-source model. Some argue it reveals a fundamental flaw. However, many others see it as a validation of the open-source ethos.

The attack was only discovered because the code was public, the mailing lists were open, and a curious developer like Andres Freund could investigate a hunch without restriction. In a closed-source environment, a similar backdoor inserted by a state-sponsored spy working at a large tech company would be virtually undetectable by the public.

"This backdoor, if anything, underlines the ethos of open source," one expert concluded. "It's only because this is an open source project that it's been picked apart, analyzed, and turned into a conversation about security at all."

The incident highlights that the greatest vulnerability is not the code, but the people. The system failed to support a critical maintainer, creating the social vulnerability that the attackers exploited. Lasse Collin gave a beautiful gift to the world, and in return, he was burned out and his gift was poisoned.

The story of the XZ backdoor is a stark reminder that the digital world is built on the passion and dedication of countless individuals. Supporting them is not just a matter of fairness, but of fundamental security.
