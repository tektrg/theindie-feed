From Skepticism to $10.1 Billion: The WHOOP Story

At just 22 years old, with no prior entrepreneurial experience or full-time job, Will Ahmed embarked on a journey to build a company at the complex intersection of hardware, software, data science, and medicine. Facing widespread skepticism and hundreds of rejections from investors, Ahmed's perseverance laid the foundation for what would become WHOOP, a company now valued at $10.1 billion and trusted by elite athletes worldwide.

The Genesis of an Obsession

Ahmed's fascination with sports and technology began in his childhood on Long Island. This passion led him to captain the men's varsity squash team at Harvard. However, his athletic pursuits revealed a personal struggle: overtraining. He would experience periods of peak fitness followed by sudden, unexplained exhaustion, a phenomenon he found deeply frustrating and personally betraying.

This personal challenge sparked a broader inquiry. Ahmed realized that while we possess extensive data about our environment and technology, our own bodies remain largely a mystery. "Why do we know so much information about the weather tomorrow or our computer or our car? But maybe the most important engine in the world, our body, we don't know anything about," he questioned. This led him to a simple yet profound question: "How do you measure the human body?"

His research involved delving into hundreds of medical papers, culminating in his own paper titled "The Feedback Tool: Measuring Intensity Recovery and Sleep." This academic exploration solidified his resolve. In the fall of his senior year at Harvard, Ahmed made the daunting decision to forgo traditional career paths in finance or consulting, opting instead to start his own company. This commitment, he admits, was one of the scariest decisions of his life, marked by an overwhelming sense of the unknown and a lack of a clear playbook.

Building the Unmeasurable

Ahmed's vision for WHOOP was not to create another step counter. He aimed to develop a wearable device that could continuously track internal bodily metrics, including sleep, strain, and recovery. "WHOOP is a wearable device that continuously measures everything about your body and sends all of that data to your phone and to the cloud where we give you all sorts of insights to make you healthier," he explained.

However, in 2012, the wearable technology market was nascent, with only about five million devices sold annually. Existing products offered superficial data, failing to capture the physiological nuances of the human body. Ahmed sought to bridge this gap, combining the accessibility of a consumer wearable with the sophisticated physiological data typically found in clinical or elite performance settings.

The path was fraught with obstacles. Hundreds of investors dismissed his idea, leading Ahmed to adopt a daily mantra of resilience: "Every day I just try to pick myself up and put one foot in front of the other and keep building." The skepticism wasn't limited to investors; the market was also becoming crowded with tech giants like Nike, and soon Apple, entering the wearable space. A prevailing narrative suggested that WHOOP should focus solely on software and algorithms, leveraging existing hardware. However, Ahmed recognized that no existing wearable could accurately measure the body's internal state.

Developing a product that met his exacting standards was a multi-year endeavor. "It took many years to build a product that I wasn't embarrassed by," he stated. The entrepreneurial challenge lay in balancing the pursuit of perfection with the practicalities of product development.

The Elite Athlete Gambit

Once a viable product was developed, Ahmed made another strategic, counterintuitive decision: to target elite athletes rather than the mass consumer market. He believed professional athletes, whose careers depended on optimal recovery, would be the ideal early adopters. "We started with professional athletes because we felt professional athletes, first of all, needed to understand sleep and recovery and strain. They were getting paid millions of dollars ultimately, based on how they recovered, but they couldn't measure recovery. So, it seemed obvious that that would be a good market."

Crucially, he also saw the potential for these high-profile users to build WHOOP's brand around performance and make health monitoring aspirational. "Professional athletes, if we could get them to really love WHOOP, would help us create a brand around performance, and make health monitoring aspirational, and make health monitoring, dare I say, cool." This strategy proved effective, with athletes like LeBron James and Michael Phelps becoming early adopters, and WHOOP's adoption spreading organically through locker rooms via word-of-mouth.

The Brink of Bankruptcy and Personal Crisis

Despite the growing brand recognition and technological prowess, the business itself was teetering on the edge. By 2017-2018, WHOOP had a strong brand and powerful technology, but had not yet established a sustainable business model. "We hadn't yet figured out how to make a real business out of it. We didn't have mass adoption yet. So, our sales numbers still weren't where they needed to be." Investors grew concerned, questioning the path to profitability.

This period of intense pressure nearly broke Ahmed. At 24, with tens of millions of dollars raised and a team of 30-40 employees, he felt overwhelmed. "I felt totally strung out in building this business. I really was very stressed. I didn't fully understand how to deal with those emotions. I was drinking too much coffee. I was drinking too much alcohol." He described himself as still a kid thrust into the CEO role without the necessary coping mechanisms.

The stress culminated in a panic attack that led to his hospitalization. This critical moment forced a profound realization: WHOOP's failure could stem not from a flawed idea, but from the founder's inability to evolve at the pace of the business. "I also realized in that moment that WHOOP could ultimately fail in large part because of me. Like if I didn't keep growing and evolving with the pace of this business, it was going to be my fault the business failed, not the idea or the technology or anything else."

Following this crisis, Ahmed committed to personal growth, incorporating meditation and stress management techniques into his routine, and consciously working to become the leader WHOOP needed.

The Subscription Pivot and Sustained Growth

However, personal transformation alone wasn't enough. WHOOP needed a viable business model. By 2017, the company was facing financial ruin, "a week away from declaring bankruptcy before we got a deal done."

In a pivotal move in May 2018, Ahmed fundamentally altered WHOOP's business model by eliminating the one-time hardware purchase. The WHOOP strap became free, bundled with a monthly membership. This shift recognized that health monitoring is an ongoing journey, not a singular event. "We realized that health monitoring was a continuous journey in people's lives, it wasn't a one-time thing. And we needed to invest in new software features and capabilities on a relentless basis, and that felt to us much more like actually a subscription or a membership than a one-time hardware sale." This membership model aligned WHOOP with its members' continuous needs, fostering a deep focus on delivering value week after week, month after month.

Expanding Horizons: Wellness and Medical Insights

Today, Ahmed envisions WHOOP as more than just a fitness tracker, seeing a blurring of lines between performance and health. "The worlds of performance and health are really blurring. Delivering this data around sleep and recovery and exercise, health alerts, even medical capabilities is something that every human needs. Great health is performance, and high-performing people want to be healthy."

In May 2025, WHOOP launched WHOOP 5.0 and WHOOP MG, introducing features like "WHOOP Age" (a measure of physiological age based on behaviors and physiology) and "Heart Screener with ECG and Blood Pressure Insights." The "WHOOP Age" feature quickly became the most screenshotted and popular in the app, indicating a strong user desire for health control and a sense of rejuvenation.

However, the introduction of features like Blood Pressure Insights also highlighted the evolving regulatory landscape. Two months after their launch, the FDA issued a warning letter, suggesting that the Blood Pressure Insights feature might require medical device clearance. WHOOP maintains its stance that these are wellness tools, underscoring the increasing complexity of navigating the space between wellness and medicine.

Currently, WHOOP boasts approximately 2.7 million members across over 200 countries. Sales commitments more than doubled in 2025, signaling robust growth. The company's latest fundraising round in March 2026 secured $575 million from a diverse group of investors, including sovereign wealth funds from the Gulf (Abu Dhabi's 2PointZero Mubadala and Qatar's QIA), U.S. health institutions like Mayo Clinic and Abbott, and prominent athletes. This funding is earmarked for accelerating research and development, technological advancements, and global expansion.

The Entrepreneurial Imperative

For Will Ahmed, the focus has shifted from survival to scaling. He emphasizes that starting a company should not be driven by glamour, but by an unyielding obsession with solving a problem. "You shouldn't start a company because you think it's glamorous. But if you see a problem in the world that you're obsessed with, and you can't stop thinking about... then you should go for it." He believes that a mission worth fighting for empowers entrepreneurs to persevere through the most challenging periods and emerge stronger on the other side.

Key Takeaways

  • Resilience is paramount: Will Ahmed faced hundreds of investor rejections and a near-bankruptcy situation, underscoring the importance of perseverance in entrepreneurship.
  • Identify a deep problem: Ahmed's obsession with understanding the human body, stemming from personal overtraining, provided the foundational motivation for WHOOP.
  • Strategic market entry: Targeting elite athletes first helped build brand credibility and aspirational value for WHOOP.
  • Adaptable business models: The pivot to a subscription-based model was crucial for WHOOP's long-term viability and alignment with customer needs.
  • Founder growth is essential: Personal well-being and continuous self-improvement are critical for founders to lead growing companies.
  • The evolving health tech landscape: WHOOP's journey highlights the increasing convergence of wellness and medical technology, and the associated regulatory considerations.
