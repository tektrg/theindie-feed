The Unfolding Utility of Intelligence: A Conversation with Sam Altman

The landscape of startups and technology has undergone a seismic shift, particularly with the advent of advanced AI. Sam Altman, reflecting on his past experiences teaching "How to Start a Startup" at Stanford, notes how dramatically the game has changed. He posits that the current era allows for unprecedented ambition and speed, where a small team with access to powerful AI tools can achieve what once required a large, highly skilled engineering force.

Scale as an Emergent Property

Altman emphasizes the concept of "scale" as a critical, yet often underestimated, factor in technological and organizational success. He observes that many of the most impactful developments in his career have stemmed from emergent properties that arise only at scale, far exceeding initial expectations. This phenomenon is evident in the scaling of AI models, the synergy within large research groups, and the network effects observed in organizations like Y Combinator.

He notes a recurring pattern: when something shows promise at a smaller scale, pushing it to unprecedented levels of scale often yields significant, unexpected benefits. This approach, however, is frequently met with skepticism. Many experts, even within AI, have doubted the continued returns of scaling, and founders often hesitate due to vague concerns. Altman argues that empirically, exploring these scaled-up possibilities has almost always led to interesting and valuable outcomes.

The challenge in scaling, he explains, lies in the fact that systems break in accelerating and unpredictable ways. Addressing these challenges requires a systems-design approach, breaking down the obstacles—technical, capital, and cultural—and tackling them systematically.

Navigating Human Systems at Scale

When scaling complex systems, the human element often proves to be the most challenging to manage. Altman highlights the importance of a clear goal, a well-defined plan, and a transparent decision-making process. He recalls the pivotal decision at OpenAI to bet heavily on scaling deep learning, a choice that required strong conviction and a clear articulation of the potential future state.

A significant hurdle is humanity's inherent difficulty in grasping exponential growth. People struggle to intuitively understand how scaling laws, revenue, or organizational complexity can continue to increase exponentially. Altman stresses the need for first-principles reasoning to help individuals understand and embrace this exponential potential.

From GPT-3 API to ChatGPT: Discovering the Killer App

The journey to ChatGPT's success illustrates the power of observing user behavior and adapting. Initially, OpenAI launched the GPT-3 API in 2020, hoping developers would build products around it, as they struggled to identify a compelling use case themselves. While the API eventually gained traction, the primary use case that emerged was copywriting, which was not particularly exciting.

However, OpenAI noticed a significant trend: users were employing their API keys not for business applications, but simply to chat with the model. This observation, coupled with the development of GPT-3.5 and improved instruction-following capabilities, led to the creation of ChatGPT. Initially intended as a research demo to encourage API adoption, it went viral, demonstrating a clear consumer demand for a conversational AI experience. This unexpected success prompted a rapid pivot, focusing on building a robust chatbot product and a business model around it.

The Rise of Codex and the Future of AI Development

OpenAI's initial focus was on coding as a key application for AI, anticipating that models could control computers and robots could control the physical world. This led to the development of Codex. While the team was confident in the models' coding abilities, the overwhelming success of ChatGPT temporarily shifted their focus.

The current AI development pipeline, involving pre-training, mid-training, post-training, and reinforcement learning, has been effective. However, Altman anticipates a significant rewrite of this pipeline in the future, suggesting that current methods are not necessarily optimal. He envisions a future where AI researchers act as interns, assisting human researchers in discovering entirely new architectures and capabilities.

Intelligence as a Utility: The Electricity Analogy

Altman draws a powerful analogy between the emergence of intelligence as a utility and the advent of electricity. Just as early electricity companies didn't initially sell "electricity" but rather "light at night," he believes the true value of AI needs to be communicated in terms of tangible benefits rather than abstract concepts.

He suggests that the current framing of selling "intelligence" may not resonate universally. Instead, the focus should be on finding relatable analogies that explain the profound impact of having readily accessible, powerful intelligence at one's disposal. This could involve a subscription model for an "intelligence token" that plugs into various applications and services.

Compute vs. Tokens: Understanding the Utility Layer

When considering AI as a utility, a distinction arises between compute (hardware like chips) and tokens (access to AI models). Altman believes that for consumers and businesses, the interaction will be closer to tokens, or even a layer above tokens. The underlying hardware and its specifics will likely be abstracted away. Users will care about accessibility, cost, and performance.

He likens this to paying for a cell phone bill, where the focus is on airtime and data, not the intricate details of the cell towers. As AI becomes more integrated into daily life, potentially through constant personal agents, the user interface for accessing these capabilities will likely evolve beyond current token-based models.

The One-Person Frontier Lab: Focusing on Inference

For students embarking on their "One-Person Frontier Lab" projects, Altman suggests focusing on the inference side of the AI stack. While significant progress is being made in training large models, he believes there is an underinvestment in making this intelligence cheap and abundant at scale. He posits that frontier labs will increasingly need to become inference companies, optimizing the delivery and accessibility of AI capabilities.

LLMs: Not a Dead End, But a Powerful Tool

Addressing the view that Large Language Models (LLMs) are a dead end, Altman firmly disagrees. He points to LLMs surpassing human intelligence in certain areas, such as discovering mathematical conjectures, while acknowledging their limitations in others, like long-horizon judgment. He believes LLMs will continue to scale and improve, and betting against their continued development is misguided. He attributes past skepticism to a generation of scientists who were too certain about the limits of scaling.

The Urgent Need for Educational Adaptation

Altman expresses concern that educational systems have not adapted quickly enough to the AI revolution. He predicted that the widespread availability of tools like ChatGPT would force a rapid redesign of curricula, emphasizing critical thinking and AI-augmented learning. However, he observes a surprising lack of systemic change in education since ChatGPT's launch. He warns that failing to adapt will lead to an atrophy of critical thinking skills.

Stanford's Impact and the "Spiciest Take"

Reflecting on his time at Stanford, Altman cherishes the broad exposure provided by introductory seminars, which offered a shallow understanding of many fields. He believes this interdisciplinary perspective was more valuable than solely focusing on his core disciplines.

His "spiciest take" is the unwavering belief that AI progress will continue exponentially, leading to a fundamentally different world. He feels this is not yet widely accepted, and if it were, society would be experiencing more profound reverberations.

Forks in the Universe: Democratization vs. Concentration

Altman outlines three likely forks in the universe over the next 10 years:

  1. Democratization of AI vs. Concentration in a Few Companies: He strongly advocates for democratizing AI, viewing it as a utility. While acknowledging the arguments for concentration based on safety and stability, he believes the risks of a few companies controlling immense wealth and power are unacceptable. He estimates an 80% probability of moving towards a more democratic path, driven by global interest, but recognizes the significant forces pushing for concentration.

  2. Distribution of Compute: He highlights compute as a critical utility and anticipates potential shortages. The equitable distribution of compute, he argues, will be a major fork, impacting economic models and access to AI.

  3. Economic Models: Altman believes that while the short-term job market disruption might be less severe than initially feared, the long-term economic models will need to adapt. He is more enthusiastic about ownership stakes (like a "citizens' wealth fund") than fixed cash dividends, as it better aligns with human psychology and the shift of leverage from labor to capital.

The Compute Shortage and Uncapped Demand

Altman confirms a significant compute shortage, with prices for high-end GPUs being substantially higher for long-term reservations compared to spot prices. He notes that demand for AI is essentially uncapped, especially if models become sufficiently smart and affordable. This suggests that compute shortages may persist as long as progress continues, as new applications and increased usage will constantly drive demand.

The conversation concludes with a sense of urgency and optimism, emphasizing the transformative potential of AI and the critical need for thoughtful development and equitable distribution.
