My name is Max, and I'm the founder and CEO of Gum Loop, an automation platform that automates about 4 million workflows every day for huge businesses like Instacart, Shopify, DoorDash, and Gusto. We're a team of 15, scaling pretty quickly.

Gum Loop lets people who really understand a problem within a business—like the marketer, the salesperson, or the ops person—automate their own work instead of having to spec it out and explain it to an engineering team to build the solution for them.

There's a category of entrepreneur who thinks they can build a business with a single click, as if it's effortless. They buy a course and believe they have the recipe for making a million dollars a year as a side hustle. This has created an anti-pattern with AI, where people boast about having 50 AI agents running their company. I think that's the wrong approach. You're just making a slot machine—or rather, slop.

Everyone on Twitter seems to claim, "I automated everything. I work one hour a week and make $10 million on the weekend with my SaaS app." Most of that is just marketing. They're lying to you. It's easy to sell hope and the vision of skipping the hard work to get straight to the value, but it will never work. The person selling you that course, however, will make a ton of money. They've found a way to print it.

Rule 1. Throw Yourself Into the Ether

I went to McGill in Montreal and studied software engineering. I really liked doing well in school, so I put a lot of effort into it, studied all the time, and tried to get the best grades I could. My goal throughout college was just to get a good job. I wanted to work in big tech because I thought that would be the right path to follow, but I realized pretty quickly that I hated it.

Many people say, "I'll just go to big tech for a little bit, learn a bunch, and then do my own thing." I think that's a form of cope. A lot of people say that, but they end up with golden handcuffs and never leave to start their own thing. I don't think I've used anything I learned in big tech in my startup at all. The only thing working in big tech provided was a sort of default respect; people see the logo on your resume and assume a company has validated your competence. But I didn't learn anything novel there. In fact, most of the things I do now are the exact opposite of how they work in big tech.

The years when you're 21, 22, or 23, with no responsibilities or obligations, are years you can never get back. If you're spending them just going to your 9-to-5, logging in, fixing a ticket, and logging off, you're wasting those really important years. I thought, if we're going to do it, why don't we just do it now? We can figure out the more reliable, responsible thing later. Throwing ourselves into the ether and seeing what worked was the right choice.

Then, I got deported from the United States and banned for five years. It wasn't for anything illegal. I quit Microsoft, moved back to Vancouver, and planned to build stuff in my bedroom for a year. One weekend, I went to visit my old roommates in Seattle. The border agents were suspicious, asking where I was going and what I did. They turned me around at the border, suspecting I planned to stay longer than the two days I claimed. That came with a five-year ban from the country.

That was the moment I realized I had to build a company because I had no fallback plan. I decided to take it more seriously and build things that people actually want—something that could make money. Honestly, I was pretty terrified. I remember driving back from the border to my girlfriend's apartment, almost in shock. It took a couple of days to settle down, but after that, it was just focus. I worked as hard as I could for the next six months.

Rule 2. Prove Yourself Wrong

I tried everything. I was trying to build anything that seemed remotely valuable. I built video game moderation software in VR, general trust and safety tooling, bot detection software for web traffic, and an anti-cam platform. I built a ton of stuff, made an MVP, and then tried to sell it to see if there was interest from the market. I did that over and over, with a different idea almost every week.

The more I did it, the more I got used to proving myself wrong. I learned the counterintuitive fact that in startups, you're actually chasing the act of proving yourself wrong. That's the best thing that can happen because you're saving weeks or months of time. In the beginning, I was building ideas for months, hoping someone would prove me right and say it was worth building. That's the opposite of what you should be doing. You should be hunting for someone to tell you why your idea won't work. If you can't find a strong reason it won't work, then you have a tangible idea to pursue.

I don't think I would have ended up building Gum Loop if I hadn't failed 10 times before. Talking to users as much as you can is a privilege you have to earn. In the beginning, you'll be begging people to try your product, which isn't fun but is necessary. Putting them at the forefront of everything, even when they say things you don't want to hear—like your product sucks or it's not solving their problem—is the most valuable feedback you can get.

I remember when I started working on the current version of Gum Loop, I stayed at the office until midnight because I was so excited. I couldn't get enough of it. When you find that thing that makes you want to work until midnight and not close your laptop, every day becomes easier, and you build this crazy momentum.

Do you remember AutoGPT? It was a super popular open-source agent framework that took the world by storm. It was the first time it felt like AI could act agentically and solve a problem on its own. I saw it on Twitter, tried it, and thought it was amazing. I joined the Discord server, which was growing exponentially, and saw people asking basic questions: "What is GitHub?" "How do I use a terminal?" "What is a dependency?" I realized if I just solved that problem for them with a nice little UI, I'd be doing something interesting.

Whenever anyone in the Discord server asked for help, I'd send them a link to AgentHub, the first version of my project. After a few days, I thought, "This could be like GitHub for agents." That idea crumbled when I realized the agents weren't actually useful. That was the aha moment. I had a platform people wanted to use, but they were frustrated because the agents were so unreliable. So, I gave them what they were secretly asking for: reliability and predictability. That naturally grew into this crazy automation platform.

Initially, it was for developers because it was an open-source project. But the audience that really went crazy for it was non-technical people—business admins, ops people, HR people—who were enthused about AI automating their work. Realizing the audience was 80% non-technical, I knew I needed to build something approachable and fun that didn't frustrate them with complexity. That was the motivation behind the simplified version.

We got into YC five months before the batch started. For those five months, the product was still completely free. During the first week of the batch, we turned on pricing at $20 a month because we couldn't imagine charging more than ChatGPT. Our first paying customer was a guy named Kai. We freaked out; it was the greatest moment ever, seeing that Stripe notification pop up. He's still a user today.

Rule 3. Real Networks Aren't Built at Cocktail Parties

I was stuck in Canada during all of YC. I had all the pressure of needing to build something amazing and live up to YC's expectations, but I didn't have any of the distractions. I was just in a small studio apartment in Vancouver, coding as fast as I could.

We learned that staying focused and not getting distracted by networking events and random tech parties is really powerful. The people who are actually building something amazing are not at those events. No one is really networking if you're onto something. I’ve kept that attitude. My co-founder almost never goes to events; most people haven't met him because he's just working all the time.

The biggest takeaway is that if you stay focused and just talk to users, your network will emerge naturally. A lot of people think fundraising requires networking to meet investors, but if you build something exceptional, they will come to you. It's not that complicated. You just have to show them that you'll succeed without them, and then you'll be the one getting the emails. A network is not made at a cocktail party.

Rule 4. Great Products Aren't Built in One Click

There's an anti-pattern with AI right now where you can go too far and become the type of person who says, "I have 50 AI agents running my company." I think that's the wrong approach. You're just making a slot machine—slop, not a slot. It's important to know what you should and shouldn't use AI for. Keeping the human touch in the important parts and automating the repetitive things is the way to go. The best users are AI-enabled, not replacing their entire job with AI.

But it's a slippery slope. Everyone on Twitter is like, "I automated everything and work one hour a week." For the most part, that's just marketing. There are a lot of "course bros" who sell the dream. People are inspired by the idea that they can make $30,000 this weekend if they just copy a workflow. I guarantee that's never worked. They're shilling the vision of productivity without offering anything novel.

The value comes when you apply AI to something you understand deeply. If there was some magic solution that would make you $30,000 in a weekend, they wouldn't be giving it to you on Twitter. The most productive people take something they really understand, apply AI to it, and scale from there.

Whenever there's a hype bubble—like with crypto, NFTs, or AI—there's a vulnerable section of the community that is easily persuaded that something will save them. You can sell hope really easily. A lot of people online take advantage of that and create fictitious content, selling the vision of skipping the hard work. It will never work. But the person selling you that course is going to make a ton of money.

I only automate the things I really understand. If you're automating something you don't understand, it's just a slot machine. If you're using AI to code and you don't know how to code, you're making malware at the end of the day. It will come back to bite you. The same goes for automating workflows. If you're trying to automate something you could never do yourself, you're going to create something that's pretty poor. I apply AI to speed myself up on things I do understand, so I can learn more and grow as a person. I'm never trying to shortcut understanding.

It's possible that the last generation of great engineers has been born. There was an era of needing to understand what's going on and then getting accelerated by AI. But now, people can skip the understanding part. There will be a much smaller community of people who actually use AI as a learning tool to understand the fundamentals. It's a slippery slope because it's so easy to not want to understand why something works. I think there will be a bigger split between people who are truly exceptional and those who are not. If you have the determination to pause, understand the problem, and have AI teach you, you'll become exceptional even faster. The average person will just fall to the slop.

Rule 5. Hiring is Like Dating

Almost everyone we've hired has come through our network. A lot of them were actually customers. Our customer from Instacart quit his job and joined us. Our customer from Webflow quit and joined us. Someone from Shopify did the same. They already had conviction. They loved the platform so much that they decided to drop everything and contribute to the mission.

It's a really fun way to hire because the only thing you really have as a startup is optimism. These people are already bought in. They use the tool every day, they love what you're doing, and they see the vision. It's a very quick transition.

Hiring is like dating. You have to be the person that someone would want to date. You can't just beg someone to join your company. You have to build something amazing and have traction to make the best people on earth want to join you. But it all starts with just doing it. My co-founder only joined because I had an early version of the product that was working, and he got excited by it.

At Gum Loop, everyone is equally excited about the mission and loves what we're doing. A big filter for how I hire is asking, "Do I actually want to spend all my time with this person?" Now we have a group of people who are all well-adjusted, fun to work with, ambitious, and intelligent. The more people you hire like that, the more exciting it gets for everyone.

There are a million reasons not to build any given startup. It's easy to hear an idea and ask, "What's the moat? Why won't X company do this?" The people who obsess over those questions will never build anything. You end up just being a pawn in a big player's game. But if you take a risk and try to prove other people wrong, you'll end up somewhere that makes people ask, "How did you get there?" The answer is just that you tried, and it worked. And when it didn't work, you tried again.

The biggest quality that makes a founder start something is thinking that they can do it. You will never start a company if you don't think you're going to be the person to do it. It just takes this blind confidence. It's pretty exciting how much someone can do when they think they can.
