Military vs. Public AI: A Tale of Two Systems

A common question is how an AI like ChatGPT, which can generate incorrect information, could be reliable for identifying military targets. The answer is that the AI available to the public is not the same as the one used by the military.

According to Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei, his company was the first to create custom models for national security purposes. The primary differences lie in the model itself and the hardware it operates on.

Custom Models and Internal Controls

Anthropic developed a bespoke model for the military, running it within a data center containing classified information. The company has also made strides in understanding the "black box" of neural networks, allowing them to control the model's ethics and behavior internally. Researchers were able to identify and manipulate specific geometric directions within the model's architecture that represent its "assistant persona," effectively placing a speed limit on personality changes.

Dedicated Hardware and Superior Performance

The military's AI runs on dedicated hardware, with entire data centers potentially allocated to a single request. This provides a massive performance advantage—potentially five or six orders of magnitude more compute power per request—compared to consumer AI systems, where capacity is shared among hundreds of millions of users.

Despite these advantages, the technology is not perfect. Paul Scharre, Executive Vice President at the Center for New American Security, emphasizes the need for human oversight: "AI gets it wrong. We still need humans to check the output of generative AI when the stakes are life and death." Even Amodei acknowledges the technology is not yet ready for fully autonomous weapon systems. Whether OpenAI can match the performance of Anthropic's custom model remains to be seen.

How AI Works in Warfare: The Maven System

Despite the risks, AI has already been deployed in conflict. The Marvin Smart system, built by Palantir and powered by a custom version of Anthropic's Claude, was instrumental in striking 1,000 targets in Iran within 24 hours.

The system processes vast amounts of classified data from satellites, surveillance, and other intelligence sources. For example, the Israeli government reportedly hacked traffic cameras in Tehran to monitor movement. The system ingested data from 179 sources in total, which Claude then interpreted to provide actionable insights.

During the initial days of the war, the system organized and prioritized Iranian targets in real time, issuing precise location coordinates. A study by Georgetown University found that the system enabled a single artillery unit of 20 people to perform the work of 2,000 staff. This fusion of the Maven system and Claude has dramatically accelerated the pace of warfare, turning weeks of planning into real-time operations.

This hybrid system was also reportedly integral to Maduro's capture, an operation that Anthropic was allegedly unaware their software was being used for. This lack of awareness became a flashpoint. An Anthropic executive contacted Palantir to inquire if their software was used in the classified raid, raising concerns that it might violate their terms of service. This alarmed the Pentagon, which realized its dependency on a single software provider that could potentially disable the system during a critical operation.

The use of AI in warfare extends beyond the United States. NATO also signed a contract with Palantir last year for its own version of the Maven system.

The Fallout Between Anthropic and the U.S. Government

The conflict began in July 2025, when Anthropic signed a $200 million contract with the Pentagon via Palantir. The Pentagon demanded that Claude be usable for "all lawful purposes" with no private sector restrictions. This was a major concern, as legislation like the Patriot Act makes domestic spying on Americans lawful.

In response, Anthropic requested two safeguards be written into the contract:

  • No mass surveillance of Americans.
  • No fully autonomous weapons making lethal decisions without a human in the loop.

On February 24th, the government issued an ultimatum: comply by 5:01 p.m. that Friday or face consequences. Dario Amodei refused, stating, "We cannot in good conscience acquiesce." The government presented language that appeared to meet their terms but contained loopholes, such as allowing actions "if the Pentagon deems it appropriate" or anything "in line with laws."

The U.S. government reacted furiously. Hours before bombing began in Iran, it banned its agencies from using Anthropic's tools, giving them six months to phase them out. It labeled Anthropic a "supply chain risk to the United States," a designation never before used for an American company, drawing criticism from a bipartisan group of senators.

Axios later reported that the surveillance aspect of the deal would have involved using Claude to analyze geolocation, web browsing data, and personal finance information on Americans, purchased from data brokers.

OpenAI and the "Quit GPT" Movement

Just hours after the fallout, Sam Altman announced that OpenAI would take the deal Anthropic had rejected, a deal he himself described as "sloppy and rushed." This move was met with an open letter from over 450 verified Google and OpenAI employees, urging their leadership to stand with Anthropic. For OpenAI, which was reportedly facing financial trouble, the military contract offered a lifeline of government funding.

Altman claimed on X that OpenAI's deal included the same safeguards Anthropic had fought for. However, given that the deal was rushed through in about two days, many were skeptical. As CNN noted, "It's not clear what is different about OpenAI's deal with the Pentagon versus what Anthropic wanted."

The public backlash was severe, materializing as the Quit GPT movement. Organizers claim that over 2.5 million people have taken action by canceling subscriptions or sharing boycott messages. The discontent also led to a real-world protest in San Francisco.

As of this writing, Anthropic is reportedly back in talks with the U.S. military, possibly to avoid the "supply chain risk" designation.

The Threat of Mass Surveillance

The use of AI for surveillance is a significant concern. A future government AI, running on massive, dedicated hardware, could analyze an entire population in real time. This scenario moves closer to a reality of "techno-feudalism," where governments have unprecedented monitoring capabilities.

This trend is not limited to government contracts. Palantir has installed surveillance systems in Australian supermarkets, and it's not difficult to imagine AI being integrated into these systems and eventually deployed wherever public cameras exist.

The Meta Ray-ban glasses represent another vector for surveillance. Meta reportedly plans to revive a facial recognition feature internally called "Name Tag," which would allow a wearer to look at a person and have Meta's AI identify them. Anyone with a public Meta profile could be identified, regardless of whether they own the glasses. An internal Meta document suggested the current political climate is opportune for this launch, as civil society groups are focused on other concerns.
