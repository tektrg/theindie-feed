The Geopolitical Oil Shock: Impact on Vietnam and the Global Economy

The global economy is facing unprecedented challenges as a major disruption in the Strait of Hormuz, a critical artery for oil transport, threatens to send prices soaring and trigger widespread economic hardship. This situation, exacerbated by geopolitical tensions, has significant implications for Vietnam, a nation increasingly positioning itself as a vital "connector economy."

The Strait of Hormuz Crisis and Global Ramifications

Approximately 20 million barrels of oil, representing about 17% of global daily demand, flow through the Strait of Hormuz. A disruption here has immediate and severe consequences. While current oil price increases are around 70-80%, historical precedents, like the 1973 oil crisis where a smaller supply disruption led to a quadrupling of prices, suggest the potential for far greater escalation.

The market's current relative calm is largely attributed to the expectation that former President Trump might de-escalate the situation, a phenomenon dubbed the "TACO paradox" (Trump Always Chickens Out paradox). However, if a de-escalation doesn't occur within the next two to three weeks, the global economy could face a hit more severe than the COVID-19 pandemic. This impact will not be limited to oil; disruptions to fertilizer, helium, and LNG supplies are also anticipated.

Vietnam's Navigational Strategy and Economic Resilience

Despite the global turmoil, Vietnam's government has demonstrated adeptness in navigating the crisis. Through diplomatic efforts, including a recent visit by the Prime Minister to Russia, Vietnam has secured additional oil supplies, potentially covering several more weeks. China has also contributed by supplying refined products to the region.

Crucially, the immediate impact on daily life in Vietnam has been less visible than in other nations. Unlike countries like the Philippines, which have declared national emergencies, or others that have closed schools, Vietnam has not experienced widespread disruptions. This is largely due to public confidence in the government's ability to manage the situation. While the overall economy will face challenges, Vietnam is expected to emerge from this period in a stronger position than many realize.

The K-Shaped Economy and Impact on Vietnamese Households

The current economic climate is likely to exacerbate the "K-shaped economy" phenomenon, where the upper-middle class fares relatively well, while the working class suffers disproportionately. In Vietnam, this is already evident with fertilizer prices doubling, placing significant strain on working families.

The cost of everyday goods is expected to rise. While the government has implemented tax cuts on petrol, diesel prices have nearly doubled. As fertilizer accounts for approximately 20% of food costs, inflation is projected to exceed 5% unless more drastic measures are taken. This inflation directly impacts those living on fixed salaries, as wages often lag behind rising prices. Businesses will need to adapt, but the primary concern lies with households whose purchasing power is eroded.

Gold Prices and Alternative Assets

In times of global uncertainty, gold has historically served as a safe haven. While initial reactions might see gold prices dip as people liquidate assets, a dramatic increase is anticipated. Some analysts predict gold prices could exceed $6,000 by the end of the year. Alternative assets like Bitcoin have also shown some resilience, though their long-term performance remains a subject of expert analysis. The underlying driver for the appreciation of alternative assets is the expectation that Western governments will resort to increased money printing to manage the economic fallout.

Vietnam's Energy Intensity and Vulnerability

Vietnam is a highly energy-intensive economy, with its energy consumption twice that of the Philippines and nearly three times that of Indonesia. This is partly due to the pervasive use of motorbikes for deliveries and transportation. While oil consumption decreased during severe lockdowns, it only saw a reduction of about 15%, highlighting the nation's reliance on energy. A forced reduction of 30% in oil consumption due to global supply constraints could lead to a significant contraction in GDP, potentially making the economic impact worse than COVID-19.

A substantial portion of Vietnam's oil is sourced from the Middle East, particularly for its heavy oil refinery financed by Kuwait and Japan. This dependence makes the Strait of Hormuz disruptions particularly impactful.

The Push for Renewables and Energy Security

In response to global energy security concerns, Vietnam has set ambitious goals to increase its renewable energy sources, aiming for 50% by 2050. Currently, renewables constitute around 10% of the energy mix, despite a recent surge in solar energy adoption. This push is partly driven by the desire to attract foreign investment, as companies like LEGO seek net-zero energy solutions. The development of direct power purchase agreements (DPPAs) allows factories to source electricity directly from solar farms, enabling them to meet sustainability targets for Western customers.

Historical Parallels: The Oil Shocks of the 1970s

The current crisis draws parallels to the oil shocks of 1973 and 1979, triggered by the Iran hostage crisis. In those instances, a 6-7% disruption in global oil supply led to a quadrupling of oil prices. The US, heavily reliant on oil imports at the time, experienced severe "stagflation" – a combination of high inflation and declining GDP growth. This historical context fuels concerns about a potential return to such an economic environment globally.

Vietnam's Strategic Petroleum Reserve and Refining Capacity

A key vulnerability for Vietnam is its limited Strategic Petroleum Reserve (SPR), estimated to hold only about 10 days' worth of oil. This contrasts sharply with countries like Thailand (10 times Vietnam's reserve) and Japan (8 months). Building a larger SPR, increasing refining capacity, and continuing the push for renewable energy are crucial steps for enhancing Vietnam's energy security.

Currency Dynamics and Trade Balance

The global demand for dollars to purchase oil is strengthening the US dollar. Vietnam, a heavily export-oriented country, typically earns dollars through its trade surplus. However, the rising cost of oil imports is expected to lead to a trade deficit in the coming months, a significant shift from its usual surplus. This, coupled with potential depreciation pressure on the Vietnamese Dong, could lead to higher interest rates, impacting GDP growth.

LNG as a Solution and US Supply Advantage

Vietnam has been advised to ramp up Liquefied Natural Gas (LNG) purchases, particularly from the US. The US has a significant advantage in LNG supply due to "associated gas" – a byproduct of oil exploration – which is sometimes priced at zero or even negative due to storage limitations. This abundance makes US LNG a viable and strategic option for Vietnam to address its energy needs and balance its trade with the US.

The Future of Oil Prices and Economic Recovery

Even if the current conflict de-escalates within weeks, the oil market is unlikely to return to pre-crisis levels immediately. The shutdown of oil wells ("shut-ins") will take months to reverse, and global demand is expected to be higher than pre-war levels as countries rebuild their strategic reserves. Analysts predict that the average oil price for the year could still hover around $87 per barrel, a significant increase from previous years.

Inflation and the Everyday Vietnamese Person

The projected inflation rate of up to 5.5% and a potential reduction in GDP growth will have tangible effects on the average Vietnamese citizen. For those with lower incomes, rising prices for essentials like coffee, transportation, and food will mean real hardship. The government is expected to implement more substantial subsidies and potentially trim personal income and VAT taxes to support vulnerable households.

Key Takeaways

  • Global Economic Shock: Disruptions in the Strait of Hormuz pose a significant threat to global oil supply, with potential for severe economic consequences exceeding those of the COVID-19 pandemic.
  • Vietnam's Resilience: Despite global challenges, Vietnam's government has implemented measures to secure energy supplies, and the country is poised to emerge stronger from the crisis.
  • Inflationary Pressures: Rising fertilizer and fuel costs will lead to increased inflation, disproportionately affecting working-class households in Vietnam.
  • Energy Security Imperative: The crisis highlights the need for Vietnam to bolster its Strategic Petroleum Reserve, expand refining capacity, and accelerate its transition to renewable energy.
  • Geopolitical Shifts: The current geopolitical landscape positions Vietnam as a crucial "connector economy," benefiting from its balanced relationships with global powers.
  • Historical Context: Past oil shocks serve as a stark reminder of the potential for stagflation and severe economic downturns.
