The End of the Traditional Design Process: A Glimpse into the Future with Anthropic's Jenny Wen

The design process that has been treated as gospel is now "basically dead," says Jenny Wen, who leads design for Claude Co-work at Anthropic. With a background that includes director-level roles at Figma and design positions at Dropbox, Square, and Shopify, Wen is living in the future of design. She argues that as engineering capabilities accelerate with AI, the role of a designer is undergoing a radical transformation. Designers no longer have the time to create beautiful, pixel-perfect mocks in isolation; instead, their focus is shifting towards execution, collaboration, and vision-setting in a rapidly changing environment.

Why the Traditional Design Process is Dead

The classic design process—a structured sequence of research, discovery, divergence, and convergence—is becoming obsolete. While it was already fading before the rise of modern AI, the ability for engineers to spin up prototypes and features almost instantly has delivered the final blow.

"As designers, we really have to let go of that process," Wen states. The speed of technological change means that long-term, two- or five-year design visions are no longer practical. The focus has shifted to a much shorter horizon. "It usually becomes a vision that's three to six months out and isn't necessarily creating this beautiful deck," Wen explains. "Sometimes it's just creating a prototype that points people in the right direction."

This shift is not driven by a top-down mandate from the design community but is a direct consequence of engineering's evolution. "The result of engineering changing a bunch is that design is sort of forced to change," she says. In this new paradigm, it's often more effective to "let them cook" and allow engineers to build scrappy versions of ideas quickly.

The Two New Types of Design Work

Wen sees the designer's role stratifying into two primary functions:

  1. Supporting Implementation and Execution: With engineers and even PMs spinning up prototypes using tools like Claude Code, a significant part of a designer's job is to support this rapid development. "A big part of the design role now is helping engineers and teams execute, not just telling them here's the design," Wen says. This involves consulting, providing feedback, and even jumping into the code to handle last-mile polish.

  2. Creating the Vision and Direction: In a world where anyone can build features quickly, a guiding vision is more critical than ever to ensure cohesion and efficiency. This "north star" prevents teams from building in random, disconnected directions. The vision itself is less about a polished presentation and more about a functional prototype or a clear direction that aligns everyone's efforts toward a common goal.

A Day in the Life of a Designer at Anthropic

The day-to-day reality for a designer at the forefront of AI is vastly different from just a few years ago. Wen estimates that while mocking and prototyping used to consume 60-70% of her time, that figure has dropped to 30-40%. The majority of her time is now spent on new, more collaborative tasks.

A significant portion of her day is dedicated to simply keeping up. At a company like Anthropic, constant developments in research, new internal prototypes, and philosophical debates about the future of AI are happening simultaneously. Staying informed is crucial for spotting upcoming trends and connecting disparate ideas.

The rest of her time is a mix of:

  • Jamming with engineers: Engaging in conversations, whiteboarding sessions, and providing real-time feedback on features they've built.
  • Working in code: Polishing UI elements, implementing small fixes, and collaborating directly with engineers in the codebase.
  • Thinking about the future: Setting aside dedicated time to visualize what the product could look like in the next three to six months to guide the team's direction.

The Designer's Toolkit: Figma, Code, and Claude

While the process has changed, the tools are a mix of old and new.

  • Figma: Contrary to the debate about its relevance, Wen insists Figma is still essential. "When I use Figma, I'm like, yes, this is what I should be using," she says. It remains unparalleled for exploring a wide breadth of options—a crucial part of good design—and for refining fine-grained visual and interaction details that are cumbersome to explore directly in code.
  • Claude Stack: As a designer at Anthropic, Wen is a power user of the company's own tools. She has shifted most of her work from Claude's chat interface to Claude Co-work, finding it better suited for the longer-running, complex tasks that dominate her workflow. She also uses Claude Code directly within her IDE (VS Code) to tweak front-end code and collaborate with the AI on implementation.
  • IDE (VS Code): An IDE has become a key tool for designers like Wen, who find it much faster to make small CSS changes or other front-end tweaks directly rather than describing the change to an AI agent.

Navigating the New Landscape

Maintaining Quality and Trust

How can designers maintain craft and quality when features are shipping at an unprecedented pace, often without direct design involvement initially? The key is to reframe the relationship with users. Launching products as "research previews" sets the expectation that the product is a work in progress.

"The promise you sort of have to make your users is, 'Hey, we're going to put it out there, but we're going to iterate,'" Wen explains. This approach builds trust through speed. By actively listening to feedback, responding to users, and continuously shipping improvements, teams can release early without degrading the brand. The real danger is releasing an early product and then letting it stagnate.

Will AI Ever Have "Taste"?

A central question is whether human skills like taste, judgment, and design intuition will remain valuable. Wen believes AI will get progressively better at these things. "We might be holding on to that a little bit too much," she cautions.

However, she argues that the human element will remain critical in decision-making and accountability. "At the end of the day, someone has to decide what is actually going to get built and what actually matters," she says. The hardest parts of building software are often not the technical implementation but the human disagreements and strategic choices. An AI can provide data and recommendations, but ultimately, "someone still needs to be accountable for the decision."

The Future of Design Leadership and Hiring

This new reality has profound implications for career paths and hiring. Wen, who was previously a Director of Design at Figma, chose to return to an Individual Contributor (IC) role at Anthropic to stay close to the rapidly changing work. She advises other design managers to do the same. "I've just picked up so many hard skills that I wouldn't have necessarily had the time to do if I was just managing," she reflects. To effectively lead a design team today, a manager must have empathy and a deep, first-hand understanding of the new workflows.

The Three Hiring Archetypes for the AI Era

When hiring designers for this new world, Wen looks for three key archetypes:

  1. The Strong Generalist: This person is exceptionally good (80th percentile) in several core skills, creating a "block-shaped" profile rather than a T-shaped one. Their versatility allows them to flex easily into the expanding roles of a modern designer, which often overlap with product management and engineering.
  2. The Deep Specialist: This is a classic T-shaped individual, but their depth of skill in one area—be it highly technical implementation or world-class visual design—is in the top 10% of the industry. This deep expertise is crucial for creating differentiated, high-quality products in a world where anyone can generate a baseline design.
  3. The Craft New Grad: This is an early-career individual who is wise beyond their years, humble, and incredibly eager to learn. Unburdened by old processes, they have a "blank slate" and can adapt quickly to new tools and methods. Wen believes this archetype is widely overlooked but immensely valuable.

For designers looking to succeed, Wen’s advice is simple: build stuff. "The best kind of 'craft new grad' folks I know are just people who just use the technology, build actual things, and don't feel limited by how little experience they might have."
