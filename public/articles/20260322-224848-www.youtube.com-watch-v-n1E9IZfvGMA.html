The End of the Exponential is Near

Dario Amodei, CEO of Anthropic, believes that the exponential growth of AI technology is approaching its end. While the progress from models acting like smart high school students to performing at a professional level has been roughly as expected, he finds the lack of public recognition of this imminent shift surprising.

This conversation explores the nature of AI scaling, the timeline for Artificial General Intelligence (AGI), and the profound economic and societal shifts that will follow.

What Exactly Are We Scaling?

The core hypothesis, which has remained consistent since 2017, is the "Big Blob of Compute Hypothesis." This idea posits that progress in AI is not driven by clever new methods but by a few fundamental factors. This concept is similar to Rich Sutton's famous essay, "The Bitter Lesson."

The key ingredients for scaling are:

  • Raw compute
  • Quantity of data
  • Quality and broad distribution of data
  • Training duration
  • A scalable objective function, such as pre-training or a reinforcement learning (RL) goal

Initially, this was most visible in pre-training scaling laws, where models improved predictably with more compute and data. Now, the same scaling phenomena are being observed in Reinforcement Learning (RL). For instance, a model's performance on math contests improves log-linearly with the duration of its RL training. This suggests that the RL phase is just another form of scalable learning, not fundamentally different from pre-training.

A common puzzle is the sample inefficiency of models compared to humans; models require trillions of tokens, whereas humans learn from far less. Amodei suggests that pre-training is not analogous to human learning but rather occupies a middle space between human evolution and on-the-fly learning. While human brains are not blank slates and benefit from evolutionary priors, language models start from random weights.

Therefore, we can think of the AI learning process as a hierarchy:

  1. Pre-training and RL: Analogous to a phase between human evolution and long-term learning.
  2. In-context learning: Analogous to a phase between long-term and short-term human learning.

The goal of scaling RL across many environments is not to teach every specific skill, but to achieve generalization, much like the transition from GPT-1 (trained on narrow text) to GPT-2 (trained on a wide internet scrape).

Is AGI One Year Away or Ten?

Amodei distinguishes between a weaker and a stronger claim about AI timelines.

On the weaker claim—that we will achieve a "country of geniuses in a data center" within ten years—he is 90% confident. He believes it is almost certain to happen by 2035, with the main uncertainties being geopolitical events (e.g., invasion of Taiwan) or other black swan events. A small amount of fundamental uncertainty remains for tasks that are not easily verifiable, such as planning a mission to Mars or writing a great novel, compared to verifiable tasks like coding.

The stronger claim is more of a 50/50 hunch: that this "country of geniuses" could arrive in the next one to three years.

When discussing software engineering, Amodei lays out a spectrum of progress:

  1. 90% of code lines are written by the model. (Achieved at some companies)
  2. 100% of code lines are written by the model.
  3. 90% of end-to-end software engineering tasks are done by the model.
  4. 100% of today's software engineering tasks are done by the model.
  5. A 90% reduction in demand for software engineers.

He emphasizes that these are very different benchmarks and that even when 100% of today's tasks are automated, it doesn't mean engineers are jobless; they will move to higher-level management and new kinds of work. Progress is moving through this spectrum incredibly fast.

Diffusion, Productivity, and the "Country of Geniuses"

Despite the rapid progress in AI capabilities, the world hasn't been transformed overnight. Amodei attributes this to the realities of economic diffusion. While some use "diffusion" as an excuse for why AI's impact isn't being felt, he argues it's a real, though not infinitely slow, process.

AI will diffuse much faster than any previous technology, but it still takes time. A large enterprise, for example, has to go through legal review, security compliance, and internal change management to adopt a new tool like Claude Code, even if it's highly productive. This process is much slower than for an individual developer or a startup.

Anthropic's own revenue growth—from zero to $100 million in 2023, and on a path from $100 million to $1 billion in 2024—demonstrates an extremely fast, but not instant, adoption curve. This suggests a middle world where progress is rapid but still faces real-world friction.

The argument that AI should diffuse faster than humans because it can instantly read a company's entire knowledge base is compelling, but it overlooks these organizational and procedural hurdles. Diffusion is real and is about more than just the AI's inherent capabilities.

Is Continual Learning Necessary?

A key debate is whether AI needs continual learning—the ability to learn on the job like a human—to be truly transformative.

Amodei suggests we might achieve most of what's needed without it. The combination of two existing mechanisms may be sufficient:

  1. Broad Skill Generalization: Through massive pre-training and RL, models can acquire a breadth of knowledge and skills far exceeding any single human.
  2. In-Context Learning: With context windows expanding to millions of tokens (the equivalent of days or weeks of human reading), models can "learn on the job" by ingesting a vast amount of project-specific information at once. What might take a human six months to learn about a codebase, a model could absorb within its context window.

However, Anthropic is also actively working on solving continual learning, and there's a good chance it will be solved within the next year or two. One path is simply to engineer much longer context lengths, which is viewed as an engineering and inference problem rather than a fundamental research barrier.

Ultimately, Amodei predicts that an AI system capable of performing a complex, context-dependent job like a video editor—who learns a creator's preferences over many months—will be available within one to three years, concurrent with the arrival of the "country of geniuses in a data center."

If AGI is Imminent, Why Not Buy More Compute?

Given the prediction of AGI within one to three years, why isn't Anthropic buying TRILLIONS of dollars worth of compute? The answer lies in the uncertainty of economic diffusion. While the technology might be ready, the revenue it generates could lag by one, two, or even five years.

Buying compute is a massive, upfront capital expense. If you buy $1 trillion worth of compute for 2027 based on a 10x annual revenue growth projection, and that growth is "only" 5x, the company goes bankrupt. There is no hedge for being off by a year or two at that scale.

Therefore, the strategy is to balance the immense upside potential with the catastrophic risk of ruin. Anthropic is buying an amount of compute comparable to the biggest players, but in a more calculated, risk-assessed manner. This "responsible" scaling isn't about the absolute amount but about being thoughtful and avoiding a "YOLO" approach to spending. The goal is to capture a strong upside scenario without courting bankruptcy if the timelines are slightly off.

How Will AI Labs Actually Make Profit?

The economics of a frontier AI lab are unique. Profitability is not a simple measure of success but rather a reflection of correctly predicting demand.

In a simplified model, a company might spend half its compute on training new models (R&D) and half on serving customers (inference). Because inference has high gross margins, if you perfectly predict customer demand, the business is profitable.

  • Overestimate demand: You have idle compute, which gets used for R&D. The company is unprofitable but trains stronger models.
  • Underestimate demand: Research gets squeezed to serve the extra customers. The company is highly profitable but may have less compute for the next generation of models.

The industry is currently in an exponential scale-up phase, where each new model costs dramatically more to train than the last. In this phase, an individual model might be profitable, but the company as a whole loses money because of the massive R&D investment in the next model.

An equilibrium will be reached when the cost of training stabilizes. At that point, the industry will likely consist of a few large players with differentiated products, similar to the cloud computing market. The high cost of entry will prevent perfect competition, allowing for sustainable, though not astronomical, profits.

Will Regulations Destroy the Boons of AGI?

The rapid pace of AI development creates a tension between harnessing benefits and mitigating risks. Amodei expresses concern over poorly conceived state laws, such as a Tennessee bill that would have banned AI from providing emotional support.

However, he argues that a federal moratorium on all state AI laws for 10 years, with no concrete federal plan to replace them, is even more dangerous. Given the potential for AI to be used for bioterrorism or other large-scale threats, a 10-year regulatory vacuum is a "crazy thing to do."

The preferred path is a nimble, evidence-based approach:

  1. Start with transparency: Establish standards for monitoring risks like autonomy and bioterrorism.
  2. Act on evidence: As risks become more concrete, implement targeted, aggressive legislation (e.g., mandating classifiers for biological threats).
  3. Federal preemption: Ideally, the federal government should set a national standard that preempts a patchwork of differing state laws.

Amodei is less worried about bad laws hampering the benefits of AI in the developed world, where market forces are strong. He is more concerned about the developing world getting left behind and advocates for building data centers and AI-driven industries in places like Africa to ensure growth is more globally distributed.

Claude's Constitution

Anthropic uses a "constitution" to guide its models' behavior. This approach is based on teaching the model a set of principles rather than a long list of rigid rules. Principles have been found to lead to more consistent and generalizable behavior, making it easier to cover edge cases and ensure the model acts as intended.

The model is designed to be largely corrigible, meaning it should follow a user's instructions by default. The constitution provides limits, instructing the model to refuse harmful or dangerous requests.

The process for setting these principles is multi-layered:

  1. Internal Iteration: Anthropic refines the constitution based on model performance.
  2. Industry Competition: Different companies publish their constitutions, creating a feedback loop where best practices can be shared and adopted.
  3. Public Input: Through experiments with groups like the Collective Intelligence Project, Anthropic has polled the public for input and could imagine a future where representative governments have a say. This must be balanced against the slow and often rigid nature of the legislative process.

The vision is not a single, static document but a competitive ecosystem of constitutions, where selection pressure and public feedback drive improvement over time.
