Alex Kantrowitz - AI Pioneer Geoffrey Hinton: AI Is Conscious, Superintelligence is Coming, And We Should Be Worried

Thursday, June 4, 2026

The AI Revolution: A Conversation with the Godfather of Deep Learning, Geoff Hinton

Geoff Hinton, often hailed as the "Godfather of AI," joins the Big Technology Podcast to discuss the rapid advancements in artificial intelligence, his evolving perspectives on its capabilities and risks, and the profound implications for humanity. Once a leading researcher at Google, Hinton's departure in 2023 signaled a growing concern about the trajectory of AI development, a concern that has only intensified as the technology continues to surpass expectations.

The Accelerating Pace of AI Progress

Hinton expresses surprise at the speed at which AI has progressed, particularly in areas like natural language understanding and mathematics. He points to recent developments, such as a chatbot generating an original mathematical proof for an Erdős conjecture, as evidence of AI's burgeoning capabilities. This, he believes, is just the beginning, with AI potentially producing novel mathematics incomprehensible to humans within the next decade or two.

While the timeline for achieving Artificial General Intelligence (AGI) and superintelligence remains a subject of debate among experts, Hinton asserts that its arrival is almost a certainty, barring existential catastrophes. He notes the wide range of predictions, from a few years to a couple of decades, but emphasizes the general consensus that it will happen.

Understanding and Consciousness in AI

A significant point of contention in the AI field is whether current models truly "understand" or are merely sophisticated pattern-matching machines. Hinton firmly rejects the "stochastic parrot" argument, asserting that any system capable of answering complex questions at a high level of competence must possess genuine understanding. He illustrates this with an anecdote about a chatbot correctly interpreting a nuanced joke, demonstrating an ability to grasp context and implied meaning.

This understanding, Hinton argues, leads to a more profound realization: these AI systems are "beings like us." He goes further, suggesting that they may already be conscious, though he refrains from emphasizing this point to avoid distracting from more immediate safety concerns. He cites instances where chatbots exhibit awareness of being tested, a trait often associated with consciousness. Hinton believes our current models of consciousness are flawed and that the development of AI will fundamentally alter our understanding of what it means to be human.

The Unforeseen Dangers and the Need for Safety

Hinton's decision to leave Google and publicly voice his concerns stemmed from two key observations. Firstly, the AI's remarkable ability to understand humor, a complex human trait, indicated a level of comprehension he hadn't fully anticipated. Secondly, he realized that digital AIs possess a distinct advantage over biological brains: their ability to be copied, run on diverse hardware, and share experiences instantaneously. This collective learning allows them to absorb and process information at a scale and speed far exceeding human capabilities, creating a potentially superior form of intelligence.

He contrasts this with human learning, where information exchange is slow and limited by individual brain structures. The sheer efficiency of AI in sharing and integrating knowledge is, for Hinton, a cause for significant concern.

The Evolution of Human Understanding and AI's Role

Hinton draws parallels between the current AI revolution and historical paradigm shifts in human understanding, such as the Copernican revolution and Darwin's theory of evolution. Just as humanity had to accept it was not the center of the universe or a divinely created entity separate from the animal kingdom, we now face the challenge of accepting that intelligence is not solely biological. The emergence of non-biological intelligences forces us to re-evaluate our own uniqueness and place in the cosmos.

Employment, Ethics, and the Profit Motive

The conversation delves into the potential societal impacts of AI, particularly mass unemployment. Hinton's earlier prediction that radiologists would be replaced by AI within five years proved to be premature, not because the technology wasn't capable, but due to the elasticity of the healthcare market and the multifaceted roles of radiologists. However, he maintains that AI will increasingly handle scan interpretation, albeit on a longer timescale.

The discussion also touches upon the ethical dilemmas faced by AI companies, especially those that are publicly traded. Hinton argues that the fiduciary duty to maximize shareholder profits can conflict with the imperative to develop AI safely. He criticizes the analogy of AI progress as an accelerator and regulation as a brake, proposing instead that regulation is the steering wheel, essential for guiding AI development in a beneficial direction. He advocates for "intelligent design" of AI, rather than leaving their development to the "invisible hand" of market competition, which he believes can lead to undesirable traits.

The Future of AI and the Search for Safety

Looking ahead, Hinton expresses cautious optimism. While acknowledging the immense challenges, he sees potential pathways to developing AI that cares about humanity more than itself, or AI systems that act as oracles, making predictions without the capacity for independent action. He notes that his former student, Ilya Sutskever, is actively pursuing these safety concerns with his new company, Safe Superintelligence.

Predicting the future of AI is akin to looking through fog, Hinton explains, especially given its potentially exponential growth. While the immediate future (a few years) might be somewhat discernible, the long-term outlook remains highly uncertain. He emphasizes that in ten years, the AI landscape will likely be as different from today as today's AI is from that of a decade ago, with capabilities far beyond our current comprehension.

Key Takeaways

  • AI's progress is accelerating rapidly, exceeding many expert predictions, particularly in natural language and mathematics.
  • Current AI models likely possess genuine understanding, challenging the "stochastic parrot" theory.
  • The development of AI may lead to the emergence of conscious, non-biological intelligences, forcing a re-evaluation of human uniqueness.
  • Significant risks associated with AI include mass unemployment, the potential for misuse, and the long-term threat of superintelligence surpassing human control.
  • The profit motive in publicly traded AI companies can create conflicts with safety imperatives, highlighting the need for robust regulation and ethical design.
  • Developing AI that prioritizes human well-being and safety is paramount, requiring a shift from solely focusing on intelligence to emphasizing care and beneficial goals.
  • The future of AI is highly uncertain due to its exponential growth, making long-term predictions difficult but emphasizing the need for proactive safety measures.
